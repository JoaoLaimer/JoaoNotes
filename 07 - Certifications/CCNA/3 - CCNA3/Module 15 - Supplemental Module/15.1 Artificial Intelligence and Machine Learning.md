# 15.1.2 The Difference Between AI and ML
**Artificial Intelligence (AI)** is an umbrella term that can be explained as the simulation of human intelligence by computer systems. This includes understanding language, recognizing patterns, solving complex problems, and learning from experience. These processes include learning, reasoning, and self-correction.

**Machine Learning (ML)** is a subset of AI. ML enables machines to mimic intelligent human behavior by learning from data without being explicitly programmed. This method allows computer systems to self-improve their performance over time through data analysis and pattern recognition without explicit programming or human intervention. ML can handle tasks like visual recognition, natural language processing (NLP), and physical actions. NLP is a branch of AI that focuses on the interaction between computers and human language, enabling machines to understand, interpret, and generate human language in a meaningful way.

#  15.1.5 Generative AI, Discriminative AI, and Neural Networks
**Generative AI** refers to artificial intelligence systems that create new content, such as text, images, music, or code, based on patterns and knowledge learned from large datasets. For example, a generative AI model trained on photos of dogs learns patterns and features from these photos. Once trained, it can generate new, realistic images of dogs, called generated sample images.

**Discriminative AI**, on the other hand, determines the probability that a data observation belongs to a given label. It predicts how closely an observation (e.g., an image or text) matches its label based on its features. For instance, a discriminative AI model trained on labeled photos of animals uses binary classification labels (0 for not a dog and 1 for a cat). This labeled data trains the model to recognize and classify new photos accurately. When given a new photo, it predicts the probability that the photo is of a dog, such as an 87% likelihood.

**Neural networks**, inspired by the human brain, consist of interconnected layers of nodes.
The input layer receives the initial data, which is processed through one or more hidden layers where the network learns to detect patterns and features. The output layer then produces the final prediction or classification. Each connection between nodes has an associated weight adjusted during training, enabling the network to make accurate predictions based on the input data.

Neural networks provide the computational framework for both generative and discriminative AI models, enabling them to learn complex patterns in data for tasks ranging from data generation to classification. Generative AI has a wide range of applications, including creating realistic images, composing music, writing text, and generating synthetic data for training other AI models. It takes a prompt or question and generates an articulate and contextually relevant response based on its training on a large dataset of text.

# 15.1.7 Large Language Models (LLMs)
GPTs (Generative Pre-trained Transformers), such as ChatGPT, exemplify generative AI that uses Large Language Models (LLMs). LLMs are advanced AI systems trained on extensive text data to understand and generate human-like language. By learning language patterns, they can predict and generate coherent, contextually relevant text based on input.

LLMs are designed to process and integrate information from multiple data types, or "modalities," such as text, images, audio, and video. By combining these data types, LLMs can perform tasks like generating images from text descriptions, interpreting visual data alongside textual information, and creating rich, contextually aware content.

### **A Note About Prompt Engineering**

An important aspect of working with generative AI today is prompt engineering. Prompt engineering involves crafting specific and strategic input prompts to optimize the AI's output. Just as a clear question can lead to a more precise answer, well-designed prompts help generative AI models understand and respond accurately to the task at hand. This process not only enhances the quality of the AI-generated content but also allows users to tap into the full potential of these advanced models, making interactions more effective and reliable.

**Generic prompt**: "How do I set up a trunk port?"

**Specific prompt**: "Provide the Cisco IOS commands to configure a trunk port on interface GigabitEthernet1/0/48 with allowed VLANs 1, 10, 20, 30, native VLAN 888, and DTP disabled."

With the first prompt, you will most likely get a detailed explanation of how to configure trunking on a Cisco switch. The second prompt will most likely get you the exact commands you need for your specific situation as well as an explanation of each command. Go ahead and try both prompts in any generative AI chatbot (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.).

# 15.1.8 Descriptive AI and Predictive AI

### **Descriptive AI**

Descriptive AI analyzes data to describe what is happening or has happened. It focuses on understanding past and present data to provide insights and a clear picture of current or historical events. This type of AI is commonly used in data analysis, business intelligence, and reporting tools.

### **Predictive AI**

Predictive AI refers to artificial intelligence systems designed to make predictions about future events or outcomes based on historical data. These systems use machine learning algorithms to analyze patterns and trends in data, then use this analysis to predict what will happen next.

### **Hallucination and RAG**
AI systems learn from vast amounts of data to make predictions and generate content, but they are not perfect. For example, even if you provide detailed and accurate prompts to a generative AI model like ChatGPT, it can still produce incorrect or misleading information. This issue, known as "hallucination," happens because the model relies on patterns in its training data rather than on factual accuracy, as shown in the example in the figure. The training data may be incorrect or outdated since re-training a model with updated data is time-consuming and resource-intensive.

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique that helps reduce hallucination and outdated answers in LLMs. RAG combines a retrieval system with a generative model. When given a prompt, the retrieval system first fetches relevant documents or information from a large database or knowledge source. Then, the generative model uses this information to generate a response.

# 15.1.11 AI and ML: Potential Issues with Biases, Transparency, Privacy and Security

AI systems can exhibit biases if they are trained on data that is not representative of all groups or contains historical biases. This can lead to unfair outcomes in various applications, from facial recognition systems misidentifying minority groups to credit scoring algorithms that disadvantage certain demographics.

Many AI systems, especially those based on complex neural networks, operate as "black boxes," where the decision-making process is not transparent. This lack of explainability makes it difficult for users to understand how decisions are made, which is crucial in sectors like healthcare and criminal justice.

AI systems often require large amounts of data to train and operate effectively. This raises concerns about privacy, especially when sensitive personal information is involved. Ensuring that data is collected, used, and stored securely while respecting user privacy is a major challenge.

As AI systems become more integrated into daily tasks, there is a risk of over-reliance on these systems. This dependency can lead to automation bias, where humans may overlook or dismiss correct solutions in favor of machine-generated decisions, even when those decisions are wrong.

AI and automation can lead to significant changes in the workforce, potentially displacing workers in certain sectors as machines take over tasks traditionally performed by humans. This raises economic and ethical questions about the best ways to manage such transitions.

AI systems are also susceptible to security risks such as data breaches, hacking, and adversarial attacks that can manipulate or mislead AI systems into making incorrect decisions.

The danger of deception in AI arises when systems are manipulated to produce misleading outputs or when they inadvertently generate false information, which can lead to misinformed decisions and erode trust in automated systems. AI can be trained to deceive, either intentionally or unintentionally. Intentional deception involves training AI systems to manipulate or produce misleading outputs. This can be seen in scenarios like generating fake images, videos (deepfakes), or misleading text that could be used in misinformation campaigns. Furthermore, as AI becomes more integrated into critical processes, deceptive AI behaviors could pose serious risks to security, privacy, and societal well-being.

# 15.1.12 Security Considerations for Using GPTs

**Public GPT Instances**
Public instances of a GPT are accessible to anyone without specific security enhancements or privacy guarantees. These versions are best used for:

- **General inquiries:** Information that is non-confidential, such as coding practices, troubleshooting generic software issues, or understanding technology concepts.
- **Learning and experimentation:** Exploring AI capabilities or generating non-sensitive content.

**What to avoid in public instances:**

- **Sensitive data:** Never input or discuss personal, financial, or operational data. This includes passwords, API keys, or any information that could compromise security if accessed by unauthorized individuals.
- **Internal processes:** Avoid discussing details about internal IT processes, security setups, or architectures that could provide a blueprint for potential security threats.

**Private GPT Instances**
Private instances are tailored for restricted use with enhanced security features. They can handle more sensitive queries under controlled access conditions. However, strict guidelines must still be followed:

- **Confidentiality:** Even in a private instance, limit the exposure of highly sensitive data. Enhanced security measures reduce risk, but data leakage can still occur through misconfigurations or security flaws.
- **Data control and monitoring:** Ensure all data shared with GPT is monitored and controlled. Use data masking and anonymization techniques to avoid direct exposure of sensitive data.

Regularly check your use of GPT to prevent accidental sharing of sensitive information. Review logs of GPT's interactions to find any security gaps or potential leaks. Regular checks help keep the information handled by GPT safe and secure.

Setting firm rules on who can use GPT helps protect sensitive data. This involves:

- **Assigning specific roles and permissions:** Limit access based on need.
- **Using strong methods to verify user identity:** Ensure only authorized users can access GPT.
- **Monitoring usage:** Keep an eye on how and where GPT is accessed to block unauthorized use.

These steps ensure that only the right people can use GPT and that it's used safely.
