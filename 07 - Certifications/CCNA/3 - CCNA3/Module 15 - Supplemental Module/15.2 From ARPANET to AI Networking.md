# 15.2.1 Evolution of Networking
### **1950 to 1980**

In the 1950s, the goal was to connect mainframe computers over leased telephone lines. This effort led to the creation of ARPANET, which on October 29, 1969, linked four university computers at UCLA, Stanford Research Institute (SRI), UC Santa Barbara (UCSB), and the University of Utah. A initial sketch of the ARPANET is shown in the figure. ARPANET used packet switching, a key technology for both ARPANET and the future Internet, showing the potential of a distributed computer network and laying the groundwork for the global Internet infrastructure of the 1970s.

### **1980 to 2000**

The 1980s saw the rise of Local Area Networks (LANs), initially using coaxial cables and Ethernet hubs. These hubs broadcast data to all devices on the network, causing collisions and limited performance. The introduction of Ethernet switches, which direct data only to the intended recipient, reduced traffic and increased efficiency. Ethernet switches, shown in the figure, are zero-configuration devices, which means the switches can learn and forward traffic without any human intervention. This development was followed by dividing larger broadcast networks into smaller networks and using routers to forward the packets within the enterprise.

### **2000 to 2010**

From the 2000s to 2010, networks expanded from simple connections of a few switches and routers to complex infrastructures with numerous devices and connections across multiple locations. This growth required complex routing schemes and configurations, traditionally managed manually. Manual configurations were labor-intensive and prone to errors, leading to inefficiencies and potential disruptions.

During this period, networks also became more complex with virtual machines (VMs), cloud computing, and the need for redundancy and resilience. The focus on cybersecurity led to integrated firewalls and intrusion detection systems, complicating network design and management. High availability and disaster recovery solutions became essential, driving the adoption of resilient network infrastructures to ensure continuous service and data integrity despite hardware failures or cyber threats. This evolution reflects the shift towards more secure, reliable, and efficient networks that support diverse and dynamic digital environments.

# 15.2.2 The Evolution of SDN
The sheer scale of modern networks made it clear that a new approach was needed to manage and configure these vast systems more efficiently. This necessity paved the way for the development of Software-Defined Networking (SDN). Around 2010, SDN emerged as a paradigm shift, decoupling the network control (the intelligence of where to send data) from the network forwarding (the actual process of sending the data). By centralizing control in a programmable software-based controller, they hope was that SDN would enable network administrators to manage the entire network as a single entity from a central point, automating configurations and adjustments, thereby reducing complexity and minimizing the potential for error.

# 15.2.3 From SDN to IBN and AI

IBN shifted the focus from managing individual network elements to expressing the desired state of the network through high-level policies and intents. This approach allows network engineers to define what they want the network to achieve, and the IBN system translates these intents into the necessary configurations automatically. This development was a response to the need for simpler, more intuitive, and less granular control mechanisms that could provide the benefits of network automation and intelligence without the burdensome complexity of early SDN technologies.

IBN and AI are closely related because AI enhances the capabilities of IBN systems. AI provides the analytics and learning capabilities necessary to automate complex decision-making processes. For example, AI can analyze historical data to predict network behavior and adjust configurations automatically to optimize performance or prevent issues. Additionally, AI can continuously learn from network activities, enabling the IBN system to adapt more effectively to changing conditions and requirements. This synergy allows IBN not only to implement configurations but also to dynamically refine and optimize them based on real-time data and learned experiences, pushing the boundaries of network automation and intelligence.

# 15.2.4 The Role of Network Automation and Programmability in AI
Network automation and programmability are fundamental aspects that complement the functionalities of IBN and AI in modern networks. It begins with **AI and ML**, which provides analytics and predictions to the **Intent-Based Networking (IBN)** system. This IBN system then offers intent and configuration to the **Network Programmability and Automation** component, which then implements intent and network monitoring in **The Network**. The network sends data back to the AI and ML system to conduct further anaylsis and predictions.

Tools like Python (Netmiko and NAPALM), Ansible, Terraform, NETCONF, and RESTCONF play crucial roles in realizing the potential of AI and IBN-driven networks. Using these tools, network engineers can implement the detailed, dynamic configurations necessary to achieve the sophisticated network behaviors that IBN and AI envision. By automating routine tasks, these tools free up resources and reduce the potential for human error, allowing IBN to focus on optimizing network performance and AI to adapt configurations dynamically based on learned network conditions and behaviors. This synergy greatly enhances operational efficiency, resilience, and the overall intelligence of network systems.
